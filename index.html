The beginning
Pablo Escobar's life began on December 1, 1949, in the town of Rionegro, located in the department of Antioquia, Colombia. He was born into a modest family, and his early years were far from indicative of the criminal empire he would later build. Escobar came from a relatively humble background, and he grew up in Medellín, one of Colombia's major cities. As a young man, he initially pursued various criminal activities, including stealing tombstones and cars. However, he soon realized the potential for immense wealth in the illegal drug trade, particularly cocaine. In the 1970s, Escobar became involved in the early stages of the cocaine trade, working with a network of criminals to smuggle the drug into the United States. He eventually rose to prominence as the leader of the Medellín Cartel, which became a dominant force in the global cocaine trade during the 1980s. Escobar's life and criminal career are characterized by a remarkable and rapid transformation from a small-time criminal to one of the most powerful and dangerous drug lords in history. His story is marked by violence, corruption, and ultimately his demise in a shootout with Colombian authorities in 1993.

The man of the people
Pablo Escobar was often portrayed by some of his supporters and himself as a "man of the people" or a Robin Hood-like figure in certain areas of Colombia. This perception was cultivated through a combination of strategic actions and propaganda, which aimed to gain the support of the local population, particularly in the impoverished areas where he operated. Key points regarding the perception of Pablo Escobar as a "man of the people" include:
1. Charitable Activities: Escobar used a portion of his drug profits to fund various charitable activities, such as building housing for the poor, constructing schools, and providing assistance to people in need. These actions were intended to create a positive image and gain loyalty among impoverished communities.
2. Popularity in Medellín: In his hometown of Medellín, Escobar was known for investing in infrastructure and social programs, which made him a popular figure among some residents. He was even elected as an alternate congressman in Colombia in 1982, although he was later disqualified due to his criminal activities.
3. Manipulation of Public Opinion: Escobar and his associates were skilled at manipulating public opinion through media and propaganda. They portrayed him as a Robin Hood-like figure who fought against the perceived oppression of the Colombian government and the United States.
4. Mixed Reactions: While some people in Colombia may have viewed Escobar as a benefactor due to his charitable endeavors, it's important to note that this perception was not universal. Many others saw through the facade and recognized the extreme violence and suffering caused by his drug empire. Law enforcement officials, rival drug traffickers, and ordinary citizens were among his many victims.
5. Legacy of Violence: Despite any perceived acts of charity, Escobar's legacy is overwhelmingly one of violence, terror, and corruption. His ruthless pursuit of power and wealth resulted in the deaths of thousands and left a lasting scar on Colombian society.
It's crucial to approach this "man of the people" image critically, as it was a deliberate attempt to manipulate public perception and gain support for a criminal enterprise responsible for immense harm. Pablo Escobar remains a highly polarizing figure in Colombia's history, with some seeing him as a folk hero and others as a ruthless criminal.

"Plata o plomo"
"Plata o plomo" is a Spanish phrase that translates to "silver or lead" in English. It is a term that was famously associated with Pablo Escobar and the Medellín Cartel during their reign in the 1980s and early 1990s.
The phrase "plata o plomo" essentially presented a choice to individuals and officials who came into contact with the cartel. It was a way of saying that people had two options:
1. **Plata (Silver):** This referred to the offer of a bribe or financial compensation. Individuals who chose "plata" would accept the cartel's money and cooperation, often turning a blind eye to their criminal activities.
2. **Plomo (Lead):** This referred to the threat of violence or death. Individuals who chose "plomo" would face the consequences of opposing the cartel or refusing their demands. This could include assassination or other forms of violent retaliation.
In essence, "plata o plomo" represented the Medellín Cartel's strategy of using a combination of bribery and violence to ensure cooperation and maintain control over various sectors of society, including law enforcement, government officials, and even ordinary citizens. It reflected the cartel's willingness to use extreme measures to protect its drug trade and criminal empire.
The phrase has since become emblematic of the ruthless tactics employed by drug cartels and organized crime groups to achieve their objectives and exert influence over their territories. It serves as a stark reminder of the dark and violent underbelly of the illegal drug trade.

A poison for society
Pablo Escobar's legacy is that of a ruthless drug lord who left a trail of violence, corruption, and devastation in his wake. His reign as the leader of the Medellín Cartel was marked by extreme brutality, including assassinations, bombings, and terror tactics. Escobar's ability to corrupt government officials and institutions further undermined the rule of law in Colombia, contributing to political instability and economic inequality. While he engaged in some charitable activities, they are widely regarded as attempts to whitewash his criminal empire. Escobar's impact extended beyond Colombia, as his drug empire flooded international markets, contributing to drug addiction and violence in many countries. His legacy serves as a cautionary tale of the destructive power of organized crime and the lasting harm it can inflict on society.
